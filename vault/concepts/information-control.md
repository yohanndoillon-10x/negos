## Overview

Information control is the art of managing the flow of knowledge between parties in a strategic interaction. Every negotiation and every diplomatic encounter is shaped by asymmetries of information — differences in what each side knows about the other's preferences, constraints, alternatives, and intentions. The party that better understands these asymmetries, and that more skillfully manages what it reveals and conceals, holds a decisive advantage. Information control encompasses not only the hoarding of secrets but also the strategic disclosure of truth, the timing of revelations, and the manipulation of the other side's uncertainty.

The concept operates at multiple levels simultaneously. At the tactical level, information control involves deciding what to say and when to say it — which facts to disclose, which questions to ask, and which silences to maintain. At the strategic level, it involves shaping the informational environment itself: controlling narratives, managing perceptions, establishing channels of communication (and back-channels), and determining what the other side believes about what you know. The most sophisticated practitioners understand that information is not merely something you possess but something you deploy, and that the act of sharing information can be as powerful as the act of withholding it.

What makes information control particularly complex is the recursive nature of strategic knowledge. It matters not only what you know, but what your counterpart knows about what you know, and what they believe you know about their knowledge. This hall-of-mirrors quality means that information control is never simply a matter of secrecy; it is a continuous game of signaling, inference, and counter-inference in which each party tries to shape the other's beliefs while protecting the integrity of its own understanding.

## Perspectives

### Thomas Schelling

[[Schelling]]'s treatment of information is among the most profound in the strategic literature, because he recognized that in many situations, having less information — or credibly appearing to have less — can be an advantage. His analysis of [[Commitment]] shows that a negotiator who cannot receive the other side's concession offer is in a stronger position than one who can, because the ability to receive information creates the obligation to respond to it. The general who burns his bridges is stronger for his ignorance of retreat options, because the enemy knows no retreat is possible.

Schelling also explored the strategic revelation of information through what he called the "coordination problem." When parties need to find a mutually acceptable outcome, someone must disclose their preferences first, and the timing and manner of that disclosure shapes the entire negotiation. His concept of [[Focal Points]] demonstrates how shared cultural knowledge — information that both sides possess and know they possess — creates natural points of agreement without any explicit communication. Information control here is not about secrecy but about leveraging common knowledge.

Perhaps most originally, Schelling analyzed situations where partial information is more useful than complete information. His work on threats and promises shows that a threat is credible only if the threatened party believes the threatener might actually carry it out — which requires some uncertainty. Perfect information would either confirm the threat as genuine (making it unnecessary) or reveal it as a bluff (making it useless). The strategic management of uncertainty — keeping the other side unsure about your intentions — is thus a sophisticated form of information control that operates by preserving ambiguity rather than eliminating it.

### Chris Voss

[[Voss]]'s approach to information control is built around the concept of [[Black Swans]] — the unknown unknowns that, once discovered, can transform the entire negotiation. In Voss's framework, every negotiation contains at least three Black Swans: pieces of information that, if uncovered, would change everything. The primary task of the negotiator is not to protect their own information but to extract the other side's — to discover what they don't even know they're hiding.

The tools Voss deploys for information extraction are deceptively simple but extraordinarily effective. [[Calibrated Questions]] — open-ended queries beginning with "how" or "what" — force the other side to engage their problem-solving faculties while inadvertently revealing their constraints, priorities, and decision-making processes. [[Mirroring]] — repeating the last few words of what someone has said — triggers an almost involuntary elaboration response. [[Labeling]] — articulating the other side's emotions — builds trust that encourages further disclosure. Each of these techniques is designed to create an asymmetry of information flow: information moves from the counterpart to the negotiator, not the reverse.

Voss is particularly attentive to the information that lies beneath the surface of explicit demands. A hostage-taker who demands a helicopter is communicating something far more important than a preference for air travel — they are revealing their desperation, their sense of being trapped, their need for an escape narrative. The skilled negotiator reads these signals and uses them to understand the true structure of the situation. For Voss, the most valuable information is almost never the information that is voluntarily offered; it is the information that leaks through tone, emotion, word choice, and the gaps between what is said and what is meant.

### Henry Kissinger

[[Kissinger]] was perhaps the twentieth century's most accomplished practitioner of information control in diplomacy, and his methods operated at every level — from the tactical management of what journalists were told to the strategic architecture of back-channel communications that bypassed entire bureaucracies. His secret opening to China was a masterclass in information compartmentalization: the State Department, Congress, and the American public were kept in the dark while Kissinger conducted parallel negotiations through Pakistani intermediaries, controlling information flow with surgical precision.

The back-channel was Kissinger's signature instrument of information control. By establishing direct, secret lines of communication with Soviet Ambassador Dobrynin, with Zhou Enlai through Pakistani channels, and with Le Duc Tho in Paris, Kissinger created informational spaces where honest exchange was possible precisely because the conversations were shielded from the distorting pressures of public scrutiny and bureaucratic politics. In these channels, both sides could explore positions, float trial balloons, and make conditional offers without the risk of public commitment — a form of information control that enabled flexibility by protecting confidentiality.

Kissinger also understood the offensive use of information — the strategic leak, the planted story, the carefully timed disclosure designed to create pressure on an adversary or to build domestic support for a diplomatic initiative. His practice of "constructive ambiguity" in drafting diplomatic agreements was itself a form of information control: by leaving key terms deliberately vague, he allowed each side to interpret the agreement in ways that served their domestic audiences, managing information not by clarifying it but by artfully obscuring it. This technique recognized that sometimes the most useful information is the information that is never made precise.

### George F. Kennan

[[Kennan]]'s approach to information control was grounded in his conviction that understanding the adversary — deeply, historically, culturally — was the foundation of effective strategy. His Long Telegram succeeded not because it contained secret intelligence but because it organized publicly available information into a coherent analytical framework that transformed American understanding of Soviet behavior. Kennan's genius was not in collecting information but in interpreting it — in reading the internal dynamics of Soviet politics with a depth and nuance that eluded most of his contemporaries.

Kennan believed that the most dangerous form of information failure was not the absence of facts but the presence of distorting frameworks. American policymakers, he argued, consistently misread Soviet behavior because they projected American assumptions onto Soviet decision-making — assuming rational cost-benefit calculation where ideology and paranoia actually dominated. His emphasis on "knowing your adversary" was an argument for a particular kind of information control: controlling the interpretive lenses through which raw intelligence was processed, ensuring that the frameworks matched the reality rather than the observer's wishful thinking.

At the institutional level, Kennan championed the Policy Planning Staff as an instrument of information synthesis — a body whose role was not to generate new intelligence but to integrate existing information into strategic assessments free from the operational pressures that distorted analysis elsewhere in the bureaucracy. His vision was of an information architecture in which the most important information flows were not secret cables but rigorous analytical products that forced policymakers to confront uncomfortable truths about the limits of American power and the complexity of Soviet motivations.

### Hans Morgenthau

[[Morgenthau]] situated information control within his broader theory of [[Power]], arguing that intelligence — the capacity to know what others do not — is one of the fundamental elements of national power. In his framework, a state's ability to gather, analyze, and act on information about other states' capabilities and intentions is as important as its military or economic resources. Power without intelligence is blind; intelligence without power is impotent. The two must work in concert.

Morgenthau was particularly concerned with the relationship between information and deception in international politics. Because the international system lacks a central authority capable of enforcing transparency, states operate in a condition of endemic uncertainty about each other's true intentions. This uncertainty creates both the incentive and the opportunity for strategic deception — the deliberate manipulation of the information available to other states in order to shape their behavior. Morgenthau saw this not as an aberration but as a structural feature of international politics, inherent in the anarchic nature of the system.

Yet Morgenthau also warned against the pathologies of excessive secrecy and information manipulation. When governments deceive not only their adversaries but also their own publics and their own decision-makers, they undermine the democratic foundations of foreign policy and risk making decisions based on their own propaganda rather than on reality. The intelligence failures that led to the Bay of Pigs and, later, to the Vietnam escalation were, for Morgenthau, illustrations of what happens when information control becomes information corruption — when the instruments designed to manage what the adversary knows begin to distort what the state itself knows.

### Robert Cialdini

[[Cialdini]]'s contribution to information control operates through the lens of persuasion psychology, revealing how the framing, timing, and scarcity of information shapes human decision-making in predictable ways. His principle of [[Scarcity]] is fundamentally about information control: when people believe that information is rare, restricted, or about to become unavailable, they assign it greater value and are more likely to act on it. The exclusive briefing, the confidential tip, the "limited-time offer" all exploit this dynamic by controlling not the content of information but its perceived availability.

The principle of [[Authority]] similarly operates through information control. People rely on perceived experts to filter and interpret complex information, deferring to authority figures not because they have independently verified the expert's claims but because the cognitive costs of doing so would be prohibitive. Cialdini's research shows that the mere trappings of expertise — titles, credentials, confident presentation — can function as information shortcuts that bypass critical evaluation. The strategic communicator who controls these signals controls how their information is received and weighted.

Cialdini's concept of [[Pre-Suasion]] extends information control to the moment before the message is delivered, demonstrating that what people attend to immediately before receiving information shapes how they process it. By controlling the attentional environment — priming certain concepts, directing focus to particular features of a situation — the persuader influences how subsequent information is interpreted without altering the information itself. This represents perhaps the subtlest form of information control: managing not what the other side knows but the cognitive context in which they process what they know.
