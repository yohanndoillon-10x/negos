## Overview

Framing is the process by which the presentation, context, and structure of information shapes how people perceive situations, evaluate options, and make decisions. The same objective reality can be experienced as a threat or an opportunity, a loss or a gain, an obligation or a choice, depending entirely on how it is framed. In negotiation and statecraft, framing is not a peripheral communication skill but a central strategic capability — because whoever controls the frame controls the terms of the debate, the range of acceptable options, and ultimately the outcome itself.

The power of framing derives from a fundamental feature of human cognition: people do not respond to objective reality but to their representation of it. Decades of research in cognitive psychology, most notably by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky, have demonstrated that the way choices are presented — as gains versus losses, as percentages versus absolute numbers, as opportunities versus threats — systematically alters preferences even when the underlying options are identical. In negotiation, this means that the art of persuasion is not only about what you propose but about how you frame what you propose, and that reframing — changing the lens through which a situation is perceived — can be more powerful than changing the situation itself.

Framing operates at multiple levels. At the micro level, it involves the choice of words, metaphors, and analogies used in a specific exchange. At the meso level, it involves the definition of the negotiation agenda — which issues are on the table, how they are grouped, and in what order they are addressed. At the macro level, it involves the overarching narrative or paradigm through which an entire conflict or relationship is understood. The most consequential framing battles are often fought at this highest level, where the contest is not over specific terms but over the fundamental nature of the situation: is this a security problem or an economic one, a question of rights or a question of interests, a zero-sum competition or a potential partnership?

## Perspectives

### Thomas Schelling

[[Schelling]]'s most enduring contribution to the study of framing is his concept of [[Focal Points]] — the idea that in situations of strategic interaction, certain outcomes become natural points of agreement not because of their intrinsic superiority but because of their prominence, salience, or cultural resonance. When two people must coordinate without communication, they gravitate toward options that "stand out" — the round number, the natural boundary, the historically significant precedent. Focal points are frames in the purest sense: they shape choice not by altering the options but by altering which options seem obvious, natural, or inevitable.

The strategic implications of focal points are profound. If the outcome of a negotiation is shaped by which options appear focal, then the party that can establish the focal point — by proposing a particular number first, by defining the relevant precedent, by framing the issue in terms that make their preferred outcome seem like the natural default — gains an enormous advantage. Schelling showed that this is why the opening move in a negotiation matters so much: not because the other side will accept it but because it establishes a reference point around which the subsequent bargaining revolves. The anchor, even if rejected, frames the negotiation.

Schelling also explored how the framing of a conflict as a game of pure competition versus a game of mixed motives fundamentally alters the dynamics. If parties perceive their interaction as purely zero-sum — my gain is your loss — they will adopt maximalist strategies and resist compromise. If the same interaction is framed as a coordination problem — we both benefit from agreement but disagree about which agreement — cooperative solutions become more accessible. The strategist who can shift the frame from competition to coordination, or vice versa, reshapes the entire strategic landscape without changing any of the objective parameters.

### Chris Voss

[[Voss]]'s approach to framing operates through the intimate mechanisms of interpersonal communication, using [[Labeling]], [[Mirroring]], and [[Calibrated Questions]] to alter how the other side perceives their situation, their options, and the negotiator's proposals. Labeling — articulating the other side's emotions and perspectives in concise declarative phrases ("it seems like this timeline is really important to you") — reframes the negotiation from a battle of positions to a collaborative exploration of concerns. By naming what the other side feels, the negotiator shifts the frame from adversarial to empathetic, creating an environment in which problem-solving becomes possible.

Mirroring — repeating the last critical words the other person has said — is a subtle reframing device that forces the speaker to elaborate, clarify, and often reconsider their own framing. When a counterpart says "we can't do that" and the negotiator mirrors with "can't do that?", the speaker is compelled to explain the constraints behind the refusal, often discovering in the process that the constraint is less absolute than their initial framing suggested. Mirroring reframes by creating space for the other side to reframe themselves — a technique that avoids the resistance that direct challenges to someone's frame inevitably provoke.

Voss's use of calibrated questions represents perhaps his most powerful framing tool. A question like "how am I supposed to do that?" reframes the negotiation entirely: it transforms the negotiator from an adversary making demands into a collaborator seeking help, and it forces the other side to engage with the practical constraints of implementation rather than defending an abstract position. The "how" question reframes from "what do you want?" to "how would this work?" — a shift from positional bargaining to joint problem-solving that occurs not through argument but through the subtle architecture of the question itself.

### Fisher & Ury

[[Fisher & Ury]]'s central contribution to negotiation theory is itself an act of reframing — the redefinition of negotiation from a contest of wills to a collaborative search for mutual gain. The entire framework of [[Principled Negotiation]] rests on the transformative reframe from positions to interests: instead of asking "what do you demand?" one asks "why do you want that?" This simple shift in frame often reveals that apparently incompatible positions rest on compatible interests, opening solution spaces that positional bargaining could never access. The classic orange-splitting example — two sisters who each want the orange but for different reasons — illustrates how reframing from positions to interests can transform a zero-sum conflict into a mutually beneficial outcome.

The technique of [[Reframing Positions as Interests]] extends to the structure of the negotiation itself. Fisher and Ury argue that the traditional adversarial framing — parties sitting across from each other, each advocating for their position — should be replaced by a problem-solving framing in which the parties sit "side by side" facing the problem. This spatial and conceptual reframe transforms the dynamic from "you versus me" to "us versus the problem," which is not merely a rhetorical device but a genuine restructuring of the cognitive and emotional landscape of the negotiation.

Fisher and Ury's concept of [[BATNA]] — the Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement — is also fundamentally a framing tool. By reframing the question from "what is the minimum I will accept?" to "what will I do if we don't reach agreement?", BATNA shifts the negotiator's attention from concession behavior to opportunity cost. This reframe is empowering because it anchors the negotiator's evaluation not in what they are giving up within the negotiation but in what they would gain outside it. A negotiator with a strong BATNA sees any offer through the frame of "is this better than my alternative?" — a frame that produces more confident, less desperate bargaining behavior.

### Robert Cialdini

[[Cialdini]]'s research provides the empirical foundation for understanding how framing operates at the psychological level, demonstrating that the context in which information is presented is often more influential than the information itself. His principle of [[Contrast]] shows that people evaluate options not in isolation but relative to adjacent alternatives: a $100 accessory seems cheap when presented alongside a $5,000 car but expensive when presented alongside a $20 book. The skilled framer manipulates these contrasts to make their preferred option seem more attractive, more reasonable, or more inevitable.

The concept of [[Anchoring]] — closely related to Schelling's focal points — demonstrates the extraordinary power of first impressions in shaping subsequent judgments. Cialdini's research confirms that the initial number proposed in a negotiation, even when both parties recognize it as arbitrary, exerts a gravitational pull on the final outcome. This is framing at its most elemental: by establishing the reference point against which all subsequent offers are evaluated, the anchor frames the entire negotiation. The party that sets the anchor controls the frame, and controlling the frame means controlling the range of outcomes that both sides perceive as reasonable.

[[Pre-Suasion]] — Cialdini's concept of the moment before the message — extends framing theory into the temporal domain. His research demonstrates that what people attend to immediately before receiving a proposal shapes how they evaluate it. By directing attention toward concepts like fairness, cooperation, or shared identity before making a request, the persuader frames the request in a context that makes acceptance more likely. This is meta-framing — the framing of the frame itself — and it represents the frontier of persuasion science: the recognition that the most powerful framing occurs not in the message but in the preparation of the audience's mind to receive it.

### Henry Kissinger

[[Kissinger]] was a master of strategic framing at the geopolitical level, consistently redefining how conflicts, relationships, and opportunities were perceived by the parties involved and by the broader international community. His opening to China was, at its most fundamental level, an act of reframing: the transformation of the Sino-American relationship from a frame of ideological enmity (two hostile systems) to a frame of strategic partnership (two powers with a shared interest in balancing the Soviet Union). This reframe did not require any change in the domestic character of either regime — it required only a change in how the relationship was conceptualized, which in turn transformed the range of possible actions.

Kissinger's Middle East diplomacy similarly depended on reframing. After the 1973 war, the dominant frame in Arab capitals was one of total conflict with Israel — a zero-sum struggle in which any negotiation was tantamount to betrayal. Kissinger's shuttle diplomacy gradually reframed the situation from a single, intractable conflict into a series of discrete, manageable problems — each one capable of incremental progress, each one building confidence for the next. This disaggregation was a framing strategy: by breaking the monolithic "Arab-Israeli conflict" into specific bilateral relationships and particular territorial disputes, Kissinger made the problem tractable without ever demanding that either side abandon its larger narrative.

Kissinger also understood the framing power of historical analogy. By consistently invoking the Concert of Europe, the Congress of Vienna, and the Metternichian balance of power as frameworks for understanding contemporary international relations, he shaped how American policymakers thought about their strategic options. The choice of analogy is itself a framing decision with enormous consequences: framing a crisis as "another Munich" calls for firmness and resolve; framing the same crisis as "another Sarajevo" calls for restraint and de-escalation. Kissinger's mastery lay in selecting and deploying the analogies that supported the policies he was pursuing, using history as a framing device to constrain and channel strategic thought.

### I. William Zartman

[[Zartman]]'s concept of the [[Formula]] represents his central contribution to the theory of framing in negotiation. In Zartman's [[Formula-Detail Approach]], the formula is the overarching frame — the shared definition of justice, principle, or framework — that the parties agree upon before turning to the specific details of implementation. The formula is not a solution but a way of seeing the problem that makes solutions possible. In the Camp David negotiations, the formula was "land for peace" — a frame that defined the trade each side would make and that structured all subsequent detail negotiations. Without the formula, the parties would be lost in an infinite space of possible arrangements; with it, the negotiation has direction and coherence.

Zartman's research demonstrates that the struggle over the formula is often the most consequential phase of a negotiation, because the party that establishes the formula controls the frame within which all details are negotiated. If the formula for a trade negotiation is "reciprocal market access," the details will be negotiated differently than if the formula is "special and differential treatment for developing countries." Each formula is a frame that privileges certain outcomes, empowers certain arguments, and marginalizes others. The fight over the formula is, in essence, a fight over the frame — and Zartman shows that this fight is often decided by which party can articulate its preferred frame most compellingly and link it to shared principles of fairness or justice.

Zartman also explores how reframing can break deadlocks in negotiations that have reached impasse. When parties are locked into incompatible frames — one side sees the conflict as a sovereignty issue while the other sees it as a human rights issue — progress becomes impossible because the parties are literally not talking about the same thing. The mediator's role, in Zartman's framework, is to propose a new formula that encompasses both parties' concerns within a single, integrative frame. This reframing does not require either side to abandon their perspective; it requires the construction of a higher-order frame that accommodates both perspectives, enabling the parties to see their different concerns as complementary dimensions of a shared problem rather than as competing definitions of what the problem is.
