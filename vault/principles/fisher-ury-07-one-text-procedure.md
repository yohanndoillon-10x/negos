## The Principle

The one-text procedure is Fisher and Ury's method for managing negotiations in which the parties are unable or unwilling to engage in direct collaborative problem-solving. It is simultaneously a mediation technique and a negotiation strategy, and it was inspired by the process that produced the Camp David Accords in 1978, when President Carter shuttled between Sadat and Begin with successive drafts of an agreement rather than allowing the two leaders to bargain directly — a process that would almost certainly have collapsed into positional warfare.

The mechanics are straightforward. A third party — or, in some versions, one of the negotiating parties acting as a facilitator — prepares a draft agreement based on their understanding of both sides' interests. This draft is not a proposal; it is a starting point. The draft is shown to each side separately with a single question: "What is wrong with this? What would you change?" The criticisms are collected, and a revised draft is prepared that attempts to address the most important concerns of both sides without sacrificing the legitimate interests of either. This revised draft is again submitted to both sides for criticism. The cycle repeats — draft, criticism, revision, draft, criticism, revision — until the mediator produces a text that, while not perfect for either side, addresses their core interests well enough that both prefer it to their BATNA. At that point, the mediator asks a final question: "Is this better than your alternative? Will you accept it?"

The genius of the one-text procedure is that it transforms the dynamics of the negotiation in several ways. First, it eliminates positional bargaining entirely. The parties never exchange positions or make concessions; they only criticize a draft that someone else has prepared. This removes the ego investment that makes positional bargaining so rigid. Second, it allows the mediator to integrate information from both sides — including information that neither side would willingly share with the other — into a solution that neither side could have designed alone. Third, it reframes the final decision as a simple binary choice — accept this text or continue with your BATNA — rather than a complex multi-dimensional negotiation. This makes it psychologically easier for both sides to say yes.

The procedure also has a powerful diagnostic function. Each round of criticism reveals more about the parties' underlying interests. The items they criticize most fiercely are the ones they care most about; the items they accept without comment are the ones that are less important. Over successive rounds, the mediator develops an increasingly precise map of both sides' priority structures — a map that enables her to design trades and compromises that maximize joint value.

## Negotiation Application

The one-text procedure is most valuable in negotiations characterized by deep distrust, multiple issues, and high emotional stakes — precisely the conditions that make direct interest-based negotiation most difficult. In international diplomacy, it is the standard method for producing complex multilateral agreements. In commercial contexts, it is useful whenever the parties are unable to engage constructively in direct negotiation — when past conflict has made communication toxic, when cultural differences make direct confrontation inappropriate, or when the sheer complexity of the issues makes bilateral bargaining impractical.

The procedure can also be used without a formal mediator. A negotiator who is having difficulty engaging the other side in principled negotiation can unilaterally adopt the one-text approach by preparing a draft agreement and inviting the other side to criticize it. "Here is one possible framework. I'm not attached to it. What would you change?" This reverses the dynamic from "what do you demand?" to "what is wrong with this?" — a question that people find much easier and less threatening to answer. Each round of criticism provides information about the other side's interests and preferences, and each revised draft moves closer to an agreement that both sides can accept.

The limitation of the one-text procedure is that it requires either a trusted mediator or a party willing to invest significant effort in drafting and revising proposals. In negotiations where no neutral third party is available and neither side is willing to take on the facilitator role, the procedure cannot be implemented. It also requires patience — the iterative cycle of draft-criticism-revision is inherently slow, and parties under time pressure may find it frustrating. But for complex, high-stakes negotiations where trust is low and the cost of failure is high, the one-text procedure remains one of the most powerful tools in the principled negotiation toolkit.
