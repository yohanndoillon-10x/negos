## The Principle

Labeling is the practice of verbally identifying and acknowledging the counterpart's emotions, typically using the phrase "It seems like..." or "It sounds like..." or "It looks like..." followed by a description of the emotion or dynamic you have observed. "It seems like you're frustrated with the timeline." "It sounds like this deal is important to your team." "It looks like you're worried about precedent." The negotiator names the emotion, then goes silent and lets the label do its work.

The neuroscience behind labeling is robust. Research by Matthew Lieberman at UCLA has demonstrated that the simple act of putting a name to a negative emotion — what psychologists call "affect labeling" — reduces activity in the amygdala and increases activity in the prefrontal cortex. In plain language: naming a negative emotion diminishes its power. The counterpart who hears their frustration accurately labeled experiences an immediate, measurable reduction in that frustration. The emotion does not disappear, but it loses its grip, and the counterpart becomes capable of engaging rationally with the substance of the negotiation. This is not folk psychology. It is a documented neurological process that operates whether the counterpart is aware of the technique or not.

Voss draws a critical distinction between labeling and asking about emotions. "Are you frustrated?" is a question. It forces the counterpart to either confirm (which can feel like an admission of weakness) or deny (which generates a false statement that both parties know is false). "It seems like you're frustrated" is a statement — a tentative, exploratory statement that does not demand confirmation. The counterpart can agree ("Yeah, this has been going on for months"), disagree ("It's not frustration exactly, it's more that we feel the timeline doesn't reflect the complexity"), or elaborate in some other way. Every response gives the negotiator more information. And the hedging language — "it seems like," not "you are" — provides the counterpart with plausible deniability. They can correct the label without feeling confronted.

Labeling works in both directions — it can defuse negative emotions and reinforce positive ones. Labeling a counterpart's fear ("It seems like you're concerned this will set a bad precedent") reduces the fear. Labeling a counterpart's positive sentiment ("It sounds like you're excited about the partnership potential") amplifies it. Skilled negotiators use both applications strategically: labeling negatives to clear obstacles and labeling positives to build momentum toward agreement.

## Negotiation Application

The most powerful application of labeling occurs early in a negotiation, when the counterpart's emotional state is most likely to be blocking rational engagement. Before any substantive proposal, the skilled negotiator scans for negative emotions — anxiety, suspicion, frustration, resentment — and labels them one by one. Each label, followed by silence, gives the counterpart the experience of being understood and the neurological relief of having their emotion named. By the time the negotiator moves to substance, the emotional barriers have been substantially reduced.

Labeling is particularly effective in situations where the counterpart is hostile or resistant. Rather than arguing against hostility or trying to overcome resistance through logic, the negotiator labels the emotion directly: "It seems like you feel this proposal doesn't respect the work your team has put in." This label accomplishes three things simultaneously. It demonstrates understanding, which reduces defensiveness. It names the specific objection, which brings it into the open where it can be addressed. And it does both without the negotiator conceding anything or agreeing that the perception is accurate. The negotiator has not said the proposal is disrespectful — only that it seems like the counterpart feels that way.

The most common mistakes with labeling are two. First, beginning the label with "I" rather than "it." "I'm hearing that you're frustrated" puts the negotiator at the center of the statement and can feel presumptuous. "It seems like there's frustration about the timeline" is depersonalized and safer. Second, failing to remain silent after delivering the label. The label needs space to land. If the negotiator immediately follows it with an argument or a proposal, the label's therapeutic effect is truncated — the counterpart does not get the experience of being heard because the conversation has already moved on. Voss is adamant: deliver the label, then be quiet. Let the counterpart respond. Their response will tell you whether the label was accurate and what to do next.

A subtler error is labeling only negative emotions. In a negotiation that is going well, labeling positive emotions — "It sounds like you see real value in this partnership" — reinforces the trajectory and makes it psychologically harder for the counterpart to reverse course. The positive label creates a small commitment, a stake in the ground that the counterpart has implicitly endorsed by not correcting it. Over the course of a negotiation, these accumulated positive labels can build significant momentum.
