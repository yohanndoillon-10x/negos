## The Principle

Interdependent decisions are the foundational unit of Schelling's analysis. The concept is deceptively simple: a decision is interdependent when its outcome depends not only on what you choose but on what the other party chooses, and the other party faces the same condition in reverse. Your best move depends on their move, and their best move depends on yours. This circular dependency is what distinguishes strategic interaction from individual optimization and what makes negotiation fundamentally different from, say, engineering or logistics.

Schelling emphasized that interdependence is not merely a theoretical property of game matrices. It is the lived reality of every negotiation, every diplomatic crisis, every competitive market, and every organizational conflict. The employer deciding what salary to offer is making an interdependent decision: the right offer depends on what the candidate will accept, which depends on what the candidate believes the employer will offer. The country deciding whether to deploy troops is making an interdependent decision: the wisdom of deployment depends on how the adversary will respond, which depends on how the adversary expects the country to respond to that response. At every level of strategic interaction, the chain of interdependence extends forward in time and inward in psychological complexity.

The practical consequence of interdependence is that unilateral optimization fails. A negotiator who calculates his "best strategy" in isolation — without modeling the other side's likely responses — is not doing strategy at all. He is doing decision analysis under the false assumption of a passive environment. Schelling's framework demands that every strategic calculation incorporate the other side's likely behavior, which in turn incorporates the other side's model of your behavior, which incorporates your model of their model, and so on. This regress does not go on forever; it terminates at focal points, conventions, reputations, and institutional constraints — the very mechanisms that Schelling spent his career analyzing.

Interdependence also means that unilateral actions have bilateral consequences. A decision to escalate does not merely impose costs on the other side; it changes the other side's calculation about future actions, potentially triggering a retaliatory spiral. A decision to concede does not merely benefit the other side; it changes the other side's expectations about what further concessions are available, potentially encouraging more aggressive demands. Every action in an interdependent system sends a signal, sets a precedent, and reshapes the strategic landscape for all future interactions. Schelling's deepest insight about interdependence is that you are never just making a decision about the immediate issue — you are always simultaneously making a decision about the relationship, the reputation, and the rules of the game.

## Negotiation Application

The recognition of interdependence transforms negotiation preparation. Instead of asking "what is my best strategy?", the Schelling-informed negotiator asks "what is my best strategy given what the other side is likely to do in response, given what they expect me to do in response to that?" This recursive thinking is demanding but essential. It requires the negotiator to model the other side's incentives, constraints, and likely reasoning as rigorously as he models his own.

In practice, this means that every negotiation move should be evaluated not only for its direct effect but for its second-order consequences. Will this offer anchor the discussion where I want it, or will it signal desperation? Will this concession build goodwill and reciprocity, or will it encourage the other side to push for more? Will this threat deter aggressive behavior, or will it provoke a retaliatory escalation? The answers depend entirely on the other side's likely interpretation and response, which depend on their model of your intentions, which depends on the history of the interaction, the reputational context, and the institutional framework.

Schelling's interdependence principle also highlights the strategic importance of predictability. In an interdependent system, a party whose behavior is predictable allows the other side to optimize against it. But a party whose behavior is completely unpredictable prevents any coordination, including the cooperative coordination that makes agreement possible. The optimal stance is selective predictability: being reliably firm on core issues (so the other side does not waste time testing your resolve) while maintaining enough flexibility on peripheral issues to allow creative problem-solving. This calibrated mix of predictability and adaptability is the behavioral manifestation of Schelling's understanding that interdependent decisions require both structure and responsiveness.
