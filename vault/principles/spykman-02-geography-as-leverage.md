## The Principle

Spykman's most foundational claim is that geography is the most permanent and most consequential determinant of national power. Governments change, economies fluctuate, armies grow and shrink, technologies transform the instruments of competition — but the geographic position of a state persists across centuries. The English Channel did not close when Napoleon fell. The Strait of Hormuz did not widen when the Ottoman Empire collapsed. The Himalayas did not lower when India gained independence. Geography sets the terms within which all other forms of power operate. It defines what is possible, what is costly, and what is practically unachievable.

For Spykman, the strategist who ignores geography is building his plans on an illusion. Military power means nothing without the geographic position from which to project it. Economic wealth means nothing without the trade routes through which to circulate it. Alliances mean nothing without the geographic logic that makes them operationally coherent. Every strategic calculation begins with the map — with an honest assessment of where you are, what your position gives you access to, and what it denies you.

This does not mean that geography determines everything. Spykman was not a crude geographic determinist. He understood that human ingenuity, political will, and technological innovation could modify geographic constraints. Air power reduced the defensive value of oceans. The Suez and Panama Canals created new maritime connections where geography had provided none. But these modifications operated within geographic limits, not in defiance of them. The canal could shorten a route; it could not move a continent. The airplane could project power across an ocean; it could not make the ocean disappear. Geography sets the boundary conditions of strategy. Everything else — policy, diplomacy, military force, economic statecraft — operates within those boundaries.

The strategic application of this insight is the concept of geographic leverage: the recognition that certain locations confer disproportionate power on whoever controls them. Chokepoints — the Strait of Malacca, the Bosporus, the Suez Canal, the Panama Canal, Gibraltar — are the most obvious examples. Control of a chokepoint gives a state the ability to permit or deny the passage of commerce and military forces, an asymmetric advantage that vastly exceeds the physical size or intrinsic value of the territory involved. But geographic leverage extends beyond chokepoints to include any position that confers structural advantage: the high ground in a military engagement, the upstream position on a shared river, the central location in a network of trade routes.

## Negotiation Application

Geography as leverage translates into one of the most practical and underappreciated dimensions of negotiation preparation: the analysis of structural position. Before any negotiation begins, the parties occupy positions that are not equally advantageous. These positions are determined not by what they say or how they argue but by the underlying structure of the situation — who has alternatives, who controls access, who sits at a junction through which value must flow.

The negotiation equivalent of a geographic chokepoint is the structural bottleneck: the party, resource, or relationship through which the deal must pass. The patent holder in a technology licensing negotiation, the landlord in a commercial lease where the tenant has built a customer base tied to the location, the sole distributor with relationships that the manufacturer cannot easily replicate — each of these occupies a chokepoint. And just as Spykman observed that small territories controlling chokepoints could exercise power far beyond their size, negotiators who occupy structural bottlenecks can exercise leverage far beyond their apparent bargaining power.

The practical implication is that serious negotiation preparation begins not with arguments, anchors, or opening positions, but with a map of the structural landscape. Where are the chokepoints? Who controls them? What are the alternative routes — and how costly are they? The negotiator who understands the structural geography of the deal knows, before a word is spoken, where the real leverage lies. Conversely, the negotiator who focuses only on argumentation and persuasion while ignoring structural position is fighting the equivalent of a land war without consulting a map.

Spykman's framework also suggests that the most powerful negotiation moves are often made before the negotiation begins — by altering the structural geography. Creating alternatives where none existed, securing relationships that create new pathways, positioning yourself at a critical junction — these are the geographic engineering moves that reshape the negotiation landscape in your favor.
