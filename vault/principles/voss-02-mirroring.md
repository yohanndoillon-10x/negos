## The Principle

Mirroring — which Voss also calls "isopraxism" — is the simplest technique in his toolkit and, paradoxically, one of the most powerful. In its most basic form, mirroring consists of repeating the last one to three words (or the most critical words) that the counterpart has just said, using an inquisitive tone. That is it. No paraphrasing, no embellishing, no adding your own interpretation. You repeat their words back to them, and then you wait.

The psychological mechanism is straightforward. Human beings are drawn to what is similar and fear what is different. When you repeat someone's words, you signal at a deep, often unconscious level that you are similar to them — that you are on the same wavelength, that you are paying attention, that you are not a threat. The counterpart's natural response is to elaborate, to explain, to provide context and detail that they would not have offered if you had simply asked a direct question. Mirroring exploits a fundamental feature of human cognition: we are uncomfortable with silence after our own words have been reflected back to us, and we fill that silence with more words — words that often contain precisely the information the negotiator needs.

What makes mirroring strategically valuable is its information-gathering function. In any negotiation, the party with more information has a structural advantage. Direct questions — "What is your budget?" "What are your alternatives?" — tend to provoke guarded, strategic responses. The counterpart knows they are being probed and calibrates their answers accordingly. Mirroring bypasses this defensive reflex because it does not feel like a question. It feels like engagement, like attention, like the natural rhythm of conversation. The counterpart reveals information not because they have been cleverly interrogated but because they feel heard and want to continue talking.

Voss discovered mirroring's power in hostage scenarios where direct questioning could escalate a crisis. A bank robber says, "We need to get out of here." The negotiator who responds with a direct question — "Where do you want to go?" — forces the subject into a decision-making mode that may accelerate the situation. The negotiator who mirrors — "Get out of here?" — invites elaboration without pressure. The subject fills the space: "Yeah, if we don't get out of here in thirty minutes, someone's going to get hurt. My partner is losing it." Now the negotiator has critical information — a timeline, the existence of a second subject, the emotional state of that subject — obtained without a single direct question.

## Negotiation Application

In business negotiation, mirroring serves three primary functions. First, it buys time. When a counterpart makes a demand or delivers information you need to process, mirroring gives you a natural pause without creating an awkward silence. "You want us to cut the price by fifteen percent?" — delivered with genuine curiosity, not sarcasm — gives you several seconds to think while the counterpart elaborates on their rationale.

Second, mirroring creates rapport at virtually no cost. Unlike concessions, which spend bargaining capital, or disclosures, which spend information capital, mirroring costs the negotiator nothing. You are not agreeing, committing, or revealing anything. You are simply reflecting the counterpart's words back to them, and yet the effect on the relationship is disproportionately positive. People universally report feeling more comfortable with and more trusting of negotiators who mirror them, even when they are consciously aware the technique is being used.

Third, and most importantly, mirroring surfaces information that the counterpart did not intend to share. When you mirror a key phrase, the counterpart almost invariably elaborates — and in that elaboration, they often reveal constraints, priorities, fears, or alternatives that they would have kept hidden if asked directly. A procurement officer says, "We need to close this by end of quarter." You mirror: "End of quarter?" The officer responds: "Yeah, our budget resets in April and if we don't spend it we lose it." You have just learned that the time pressure is on their side, not yours — a critical piece of leverage that reshapes the entire negotiation.

The most common mistake with mirroring is overdoing it. Used once or twice in a conversation, mirroring feels natural and facilitating. Used five or six times, it feels robotic and manipulative. Voss recommends using mirroring sparingly and combining it with other techniques — following a mirror with a label, or pairing it with a calibrated question — to maintain the conversational flow. The other common error is mirroring with the wrong tone. A mirror delivered in a flat or aggressive tone sounds like mockery. The tone must convey genuine curiosity — a rising inflection that says "tell me more" without explicitly asking for it.
