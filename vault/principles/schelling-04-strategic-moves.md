## The Principle

Schelling drew a fundamental distinction between ordinary moves and strategic moves. An ordinary move is a choice made within the structure of a given interaction — accepting an offer, rejecting a counteroffer, setting a price. A strategic move is an action that changes the structure of the interaction itself before the ordinary moves begin. The difference is between playing the game and changing the game. Strategic moves include commitments, threats, promises, and any action that alters the payoffs, the sequence, or the information available to the parties. They are, in Schelling's framework, the most consequential decisions a negotiator makes — and they typically occur before the formal negotiation begins.

The logic is straightforward. In any strategic interaction, the outcome depends on the choices of all parties. But the choices each party makes depend on what it expects the other to do. A strategic move works by changing those expectations. If I make a credible commitment to a certain position, I have changed your expectation about my future behavior, and therefore changed the calculation you must make about your own best move. If I make a credible threat — "I will impose these costs on you if you do not comply" — I have introduced a new contingency into your decision calculus. If I make a credible promise — "I will reward you if you cooperate" — I have done the same. In each case, the strategic move works not by directly producing a favorable outcome but by restructuring the strategic environment so that a favorable outcome becomes the other side's rational choice.

The critical distinction between strategic moves and bluffing is credibility. A bluff is a claim about future behavior that the claimant does not intend to follow through on. A strategic move is a genuine alteration of the strategic landscape — a commitment that is binding, a threat that will be executed, a promise that will be honored. The difference matters enormously because sophisticated counterparts will test the credibility of strategic moves, and moves that fail the test are worse than useless: they destroy the mover's credibility for future interactions.

Schelling identified several categories of strategic moves. Commitments bind the mover to a specific course of action regardless of what the other side does. Threats make the mover's future action contingent on the other side's behavior in a punitive direction. Promises make it contingent in a rewarding direction. Warnings convey information about consequences without necessarily committing to a response. Each type has its own logic, its own credibility requirements, and its own risks. The art of strategic negotiation, in Schelling's framework, is the art of choosing the right type of strategic move for the situation, constructing it so that it is credible, and deploying it at the right moment.

## Negotiation Application

In practice, strategic moves manifest as pre-negotiation positioning. The company that publicly announces its acquisition criteria before entering a deal process has made a strategic move: it has anchored expectations and constrained its own flexibility in ways that shape the subsequent negotiation. The government that passes legislation mandating certain terms in trade agreements has made a strategic move: it has created an institutional constraint that its negotiators can point to as immovable. The individual who obtains a competing job offer before negotiating a raise has made a strategic move: the offer alters the employer's calculation of the cost of saying no.

The timing of strategic moves is critical. A move made too early may be forgotten or its credibility may erode. A move made too late may be seen as reactive rather than structural. The ideal strategic move is one that is visible to the other side, clearly irreversible (or costly to reverse), and established before the substantive negotiation begins. Schelling's framework teaches that the negotiator who spends more time shaping the strategic environment and less time arguing about positions within it will consistently achieve better outcomes.
