## The Principle

Of all the distinctions Voss draws, the one between "that's right" and "you're right" may be the most subtle and the most consequential. When a counterpart says "that's right" in response to something you have said, it signals a genuine breakthrough — a moment of authentic recognition in which the counterpart feels that their perspective, their situation, their emotional reality has been fully understood. "That's right" is an involuntary acknowledgment. It cannot be faked or extracted through pressure. It emerges only when the counterpart's deepest concerns have been accurately articulated back to them.

"You're right," by contrast, is a conversational dead end. It is what people say when they want you to stop talking. A child says "you're right" to a lecturing parent. An employee says "you're right" to a manager they have stopped listening to. A negotiation counterpart says "you're right" when they want to end an uncomfortable line of discussion without actually agreeing to anything. "You're right" does not indicate understanding or buy-in. It indicates capitulation to conversational pressure — and capitulation, unlike genuine agreement, does not survive the end of the conversation. A counterpart who says "you're right" and agrees to terms will quietly fail to implement them, renegotiate at the next opportunity, or simply disappear.

Voss discovered this distinction through a specific hostage negotiation in which an apparent breakthrough — the subject agreed with everything the negotiator said — turned out to be entirely superficial. The subject said "you're right" to end the conversation, not to signal genuine understanding. The negotiation nearly collapsed because the team mistook conversational compliance for real agreement. Voss subsequently began training his teams to listen for "that's right" as the indicator of a genuine shift and to treat "you're right" as a warning sign that the counterpart has disengaged.

The practical question, then, is how to elicit "that's right" rather than "you're right." The answer involves a specific technique Voss calls a "summary" — a combination of paraphrasing (restating the counterpart's words) and labeling (naming the emotions behind those words). The negotiator summarizes the counterpart's position and the emotional context surrounding it, delivering a comprehensive, empathetic account of how the world looks from the other side of the table. When this summary is accurate enough, the counterpart's natural response is "that's right" — an involuntary recognition that they have been genuinely understood.

## Negotiation Application

The pursuit of "that's right" should be a deliberate strategic objective, not a happy accident. During preparation, the negotiator should draft a summary of the counterpart's situation — their constraints, their pressures, their fears, their aspirations — as the counterpart would describe it. This summary should be empathetic without being sympathetic: it should demonstrate understanding of the counterpart's perspective without endorsing it. The negotiator then looks for an appropriate moment in the negotiation to deliver this summary, typically after enough mirroring and labeling have established a foundation of rapport.

The moment the counterpart says "that's right" is a strategic inflection point. It indicates that the counterpart's defensive barriers have been lowered and that they are now psychologically prepared to consider proposals that they would have rejected moments earlier. The skilled negotiator recognizes this moment and uses it — delivering a key proposal, raising a difficult topic, or introducing a calibrated question that advances the negotiation toward the desired outcome. Timing the substantive move to follow a "that's right" moment dramatically increases the probability that the proposal will be seriously considered.

The most common mistake is trying to accelerate "that's right" by arguing the counterpart into agreement. Logic, data, and persuasive arguments do not produce "that's right" — they produce "you're right." The counterpart says "you're right" not because they agree but because they have been overwhelmed by the force of the argument and want to move on. The negotiator feels victorious; the counterpart feels silenced. The agreement that follows is hollow. Voss's advice is brutally simple: stop trying to be right. Start trying to understand. "That's right" comes not from proving your point but from proving that you understand theirs.

A second subtle error is failing to distinguish between "that's right" and simple agreement. Not every nod or "yes" carries the weight of a "that's right" moment. The genuine article has a specific quality — a sense of emotional release, a moment where the counterpart visibly relaxes, where the temperature in the room shifts. Experienced practitioners learn to recognize this quality and to calibrate their next move accordingly. Moving to substance after a polite "yes" rather than a genuine "that's right" is premature and can undo progress that has been carefully built.
