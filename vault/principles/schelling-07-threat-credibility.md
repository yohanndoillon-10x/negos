## The Principle

A threat is only as powerful as the other side's belief that it will be carried out. Schelling's analysis of threat credibility begins with a simple observation: making a threat is easy; making it believed is hard. The difficulty arises because carrying out a threat is usually costly to the threatener as well as the target. A company that threatens to litigate rather than settle will incur legal costs. A nation that threatens military retaliation will suffer casualties. A negotiator who threatens to walk away from a deal loses the deal. In every case, the target of the threat knows that following through will hurt the threatener, and this knowledge undermines the credibility of the threat.

Schelling's framework identifies several mechanisms for establishing threat credibility. The first is reputation: a party that has carried out threats in the past is more likely to be believed in the future. Reputation is a slow-building asset that compounds over time, and it is one reason why experienced negotiators are careful about which threats they make — because every unfulfilled threat erodes the credibility of future ones. The second mechanism is commitment: a party that has taken visible steps that would make it costly not to follow through — public statements, institutional preparations, sunk costs — has made its threat more credible. The third is passion or irrationality: a party that appears genuinely angry, emotionally invested, or slightly irrational may be believed precisely because rational actors would not follow through, but this actor might. Schelling noted the paradox that a reputation for being somewhat unpredictable can be a strategic asset.

The deepest insight in Schelling's analysis of threat credibility is the distinction between deterrent threats and compellent threats. A deterrent threat says: "If you change the status quo, I will punish you." A compellent threat says: "If you do not change the status quo, I will punish you." Deterrent threats are inherently easier to make credible because they require the threatener to act only if the target acts first. Compellent threats are harder because they require the threatener to initiate costly action, and the target can simply wait and see whether the threatener follows through. This asymmetry has profound implications for negotiation strategy: it is generally easier to defend an existing position than to coerce the other side into changing theirs.

The relationship between threat credibility and proportionality is also critical. A threat that is disproportionate to the issue at stake undermines itself. If a supplier threatens to terminate a billion-dollar relationship over a minor contractual dispute, the threat lacks credibility precisely because the cost to the threatener is so obviously out of proportion to the grievance. Credible threats are calibrated: the threatened response must be proportionate to the provocation, and the threatened party must believe that carrying it out would be a rational (if regrettable) response.

## Negotiation Application

The practical implications are direct. First, never make a threat you are not prepared to carry out. Every empty threat depletes your credibility bank account, making future threats less effective regardless of whether they are genuine. Second, invest in the infrastructure of credibility before you need it. Build a reputation for following through on commitments. Take visible preparatory steps that signal seriousness — retain counsel, prepare alternative supplier relationships, begin regulatory filings. Third, calibrate your threats to the situation. A threat that is proportionate and specific is far more credible than one that is sweeping and dramatic. "We will file a breach-of-contract claim for the specific damages incurred" is more credible than "We will destroy your business."

Finally, Schelling's framework teaches the value of partial execution. Rather than threatening a massive retaliatory action, a party can take a small, costly step that demonstrates willingness to follow through. Filing a preliminary motion rather than a full lawsuit. Withdrawing from one project rather than the entire relationship. These partial actions serve as credibility signals — proof that the threatener is willing to bear costs — and they make the full threat more believable without requiring its complete execution.
