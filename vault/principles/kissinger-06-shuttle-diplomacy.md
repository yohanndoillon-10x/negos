## The Principle

Shuttle diplomacy is the practice of conducting negotiations by traveling between parties who cannot or will not negotiate directly, serving as the sole channel of communication and the interpreter of each side's positions to the other. Kissinger perfected this technique during the Middle East negotiations following the 1973 Yom Kippur War, flying repeatedly between Jerusalem, Cairo, and Damascus to broker disengagement agreements between Israel, Egypt, and Syria. The parties could not sit across a table from each other — the political constraints, the depth of mutual hostility, the domestic consequences of appearing to negotiate with the enemy — made direct talks impossible. Kissinger became the table.

The strategic power of shuttle diplomacy derives from information control. The mediator who is the sole conduit between the parties controls what each side knows about the other's positions, priorities, flexibility, and constraints. Kissinger could frame each side's proposals in ways that maximized their appeal to the other, soften ultimatums before delivering them, test ideas without commitment ("What if, hypothetically, the other side were willing to..."), and manage the pace of concessions to create momentum. He was not a neutral mediator in the traditional sense. He was an active participant with his own strategic objectives — American interests in regional stability, containment of Soviet influence, preservation of the Israeli relationship — and the shuttle format gave him the power to pursue those objectives while appearing to serve as an honest broker.

The technique works because it solves several problems simultaneously. It allows parties to negotiate without the political cost of direct engagement. It provides a face-saving mechanism, because concessions are made to the mediator rather than to the adversary. It manages the information environment, preventing the kind of public posturing and escalatory rhetoric that accompanies open negotiations. And it gives the mediator enormous leverage, because each side depends on him for accurate information about the other's positions — information he can shade, sequence, and frame to serve the broader objective.

## Negotiation Application

In business contexts, shuttle diplomacy occurs whenever a mediator, broker, investment banker, real estate agent, or intermediary carries proposals between parties who do not negotiate directly. The dynamics are identical to Kissinger's Middle East shuttles: the intermediary controls the information flow, frames proposals, manages expectations, and has the power to shape outcomes through the selective presentation of each side's positions.

The negotiator who finds himself in the shuttle position holds extraordinary power and must exercise it with care. The temptation to shade information — to tell each side what it wants to hear, to exaggerate the other side's flexibility, to minimize the gap between positions — is enormous and sometimes productive in the short term. But a mediator who is caught manipulating information loses credibility with both sides, and once credibility is lost, the shuttle collapses. Kissinger maintained credibility by being scrupulously accurate about facts while being strategically creative about framing. He would not misrepresent what the other side had said, but he would present it in the context most likely to elicit a constructive response.

The negotiator who is a party rather than the mediator must be alert to the risks of shuttle dynamics. When someone else controls the flow of information between you and your counterpart, you are at a structural disadvantage. The intermediary may be framing your proposals in ways you would not choose, filtering the other side's responses, or pursuing objectives that diverge from your own. The countermeasure is to insist on direct channels of communication — even informal ones — that allow you to verify the intermediary's representations and maintain your own relationship with the other side.
