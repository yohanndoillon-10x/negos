## The Principle

Once people take a stand — make a choice, state a position, perform an action — they experience intense internal and external pressure to behave consistently with that commitment. This is not merely a matter of social appearance, though the desire to appear consistent to others is part of it. The deeper mechanism is cognitive: inconsistency creates psychological discomfort (what Festinger called cognitive dissonance), and humans are strongly motivated to reduce that discomfort by aligning subsequent behavior with prior commitments. Consistency is, under normal circumstances, an adaptive and valued trait. The person who says one thing and does another is judged unreliable, erratic, untrustworthy. The person whose words and actions align is judged rational, stable, and honest. This social valuation has been internalized so deeply that consistency becomes an automatic response — a shortcut that saves the effort of rethinking every decision from scratch.

The exploitative potential arises because the commitment need not be large or significant to trigger the consistency drive. Small, seemingly inconsequential commitments create powerful momentum toward larger ones. This is the mechanism behind the "foot-in-the-door" technique, one of the most robustly replicated findings in social psychology. Freedman and Fraser's classic 1966 study demonstrated that homeowners who agreed to place a small sign in their window ("Be a Safe Driver") were subsequently four times more likely to agree to a large, ugly billboard on their lawn than homeowners who were approached with only the large request. The small initial commitment altered their self-image — "I am the kind of person who supports public safety campaigns" — and consistency demanded that they act in accordance with this identity when the larger request came.

Cialdini identified several factors that amplify the power of commitment. Commitments that are active (written or spoken rather than merely thought), public (made in front of others), effortful (requiring work or sacrifice), and perceived as freely chosen (not coerced) create the strongest consistency pressure. The car dealership's lowball technique exploits this architecture systematically: the customer actively chooses a car, fills out paperwork (active, effortful commitment), tells friends and family about the purchase (public), and experiences the choice as voluntary. When the salesman returns with a higher price — the original deal having mysteriously fallen through — the customer's entire consistency apparatus is engaged. Walking away now means reversing an active, public, effortful, voluntary commitment. Most people pay the higher price rather than endure that reversal.

## Negotiation Application

In negotiation, commitment and consistency operates as both a binding mechanism and a momentum generator. The skilled negotiator understands how to elicit small commitments early in the process that build toward larger ones, and how to protect themselves from the same technique.

Offensively, the principle suggests starting with requests for small, easy agreements — points of common ground, procedural commitments, statements of shared interest. Each agreement, no matter how minor, shifts the counterpart's self-concept slightly toward "I am someone who cooperates with this person" and "I am someone who is moving toward a deal." These micro-commitments accumulate. By the time the negotiation reaches the difficult substantive issues, the counterpart has established a pattern of agreement that consistency pressure will work to maintain. This is not deception — the early agreements should be genuine — but it is strategic sequencing designed to harness a known psychological dynamic.

The principle also explains why getting commitments in writing is so important. A verbal agreement to "think about it" creates weak consistency pressure. A signed term sheet, even a non-binding one, creates dramatically stronger pressure because it is active, effortful, and documented. Negotiators who understand this principle insist on written summaries of each session's agreements, not just for the legal record but because the act of signing or confirming in writing deepens the counterpart's psychological investment in the trajectory.

Defensively, recognizing the commitment-consistency dynamic is essential when you feel reluctant to back away from a position or a deal that is no longer serving your interests. The question to ask is: "Am I continuing because the deal is genuinely good, or because I have invested too much to feel comfortable reversing course?" If the answer is the latter, you are experiencing consistency pressure, not rational analysis. Cialdini's defense is to separate the current decision from past commitments — to ask, "Knowing what I know now, would I make this commitment again from scratch?" If the answer is no, consistency is a trap, not a guide.
