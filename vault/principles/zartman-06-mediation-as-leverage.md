## The Principle

Zartman challenged the conventional wisdom that mediators are — or should be — neutral facilitators whose only role is to help parties communicate more effectively. His research demonstrated that the most consequential mediations in international affairs have been conducted by parties who brought their own interests, their own resources, and their own leverage to the table. The United States did not mediate the Camp David Accords as a disinterested bystander; it mediated as a superpower with its own strategic interests in Middle Eastern stability, its own relationships with both Egypt and Israel, and its own capacity to offer inducements and threaten consequences. The mediator, in Zartman's framework, is not outside the negotiation. The mediator is a third party to the negotiation — one whose interests, capabilities, and strategic choices profoundly shape the outcome.

Zartman identified three modes of mediation, arranged along a spectrum of increasing involvement. The first is communication facilitation, in which the mediator simply helps the parties exchange information and proposals. The second is formulation, in which the mediator actively proposes frameworks, reframes issues, and suggests trade-offs that the parties have not considered. The third is manipulation, in which the mediator uses its own resources to alter the incentive structure of the negotiation — offering side-payments to make agreement more attractive, threatening consequences to make disagreement more costly, or setting deadlines that create urgency. The most effective mediators, Zartman argued, operate across all three modes, calibrating their level of involvement to the needs of the moment.

The implication of this analysis is that the selection of a mediator is itself a strategic decision with profound consequences for the outcome of the negotiation. A mediator with resources and leverage can break deadlocks that a facilitator cannot. But a mediator with its own interests may also distort the negotiation in ways that serve the mediator's agenda at the expense of the parties'. The parties must therefore weigh the mediator's capacity to deliver results against the mediator's potential to shape the outcome in self-serving ways. This calculus is one of the most consequential and least discussed aspects of international conflict resolution.

## Negotiation Application

The mediation-as-leverage principle has immediate practical implications for anyone involved in mediated disputes, whether in international affairs, commercial arbitration, or organizational conflict resolution. For parties considering mediation, the principle counsels careful attention to mediator selection. The question is not simply "who is neutral?" but "who has the capacity and the incentive to produce an outcome we can live with?" A mediator who lacks leverage may be perfectly fair but perfectly ineffective. A mediator who has leverage but whose interests diverge from yours may produce an efficient outcome that does not serve your needs.

For mediators themselves, Zartman's framework provides a sophisticated understanding of the tools at their disposal. Communication facilitation is the baseline, but it is often insufficient for conflicts that have resisted resolution. The mediator who confines himself to restating each side's position and encouraging empathy will find that deeply entrenched conflicts do not yield to good process alone. The willingness to propose formulas — to put ideas on the table that neither side has articulated — is often the intervention that breaks a deadlock. And in the most difficult cases, the willingness to use leverage — to make the parties understand that the mediator's continued engagement, or the benefits that flow from the mediator's relationship, are contingent on good-faith negotiation — can be the decisive factor.

The principle also illuminates the dynamics of informal mediation that occurs in every organizational context. A manager mediating a dispute between two subordinates brings her own authority, her own preferences, and her own ability to allocate resources. A board member mediating between a CEO and a major shareholder brings his own network, reputation, and capacity to influence other stakeholders. Recognizing that mediation is never purely neutral — that every mediator brings something to the table and wants something from it — is the first step toward managing the mediation process strategically rather than naively.
