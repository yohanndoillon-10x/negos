## The Principle

Negotiation jujitsu is Fisher and Ury's strategy for dealing with counterparts who refuse to engage in principled negotiation and insist on positional bargaining. The name is deliberately evocative: just as the martial art of jujitsu redirects an attacker's force rather than opposing it directly, negotiation jujitsu redirects positional tactics into interest-based discussion without escalating the conflict. The underlying insight is that pushing back against positional bargaining with your own positional tactics simply reinforces the adversarial dynamic, while yielding to positional pressure sacrifices your interests. The third option — neither pushing back nor giving in — is to sidestep the attack and redirect the energy toward the problem.

Fisher and Ury identified three common positional tactics and prescribed specific responses for each. When the other side asserts their position forcefully, do not reject it or accept it — treat it as one possible option and ask about the interests behind it. "That's an interesting proposal. Help me understand what concerns it addresses for you." When the other side attacks your ideas, do not defend them — invite criticism. "What concerns do you see with this approach? What would you improve?" This is counterintuitive but powerful: by asking the other side to critique your proposal, you obtain valuable information about their interests and make them feel heard, which reduces their need to attack. When the other side attacks you personally, do not counterattack — recast the personal attack as an expression of the problem and redirect to substance. "It sounds like you're frustrated with the pace of these discussions. Let's focus on what we can do to move things forward."

The deeper logic of negotiation jujitsu is about controlling the frame of the interaction. Every negotiation operates within an implicit frame — a shared understanding of what kind of interaction is taking place. Positional bargaining frames the interaction as a contest: I win if you lose. Principled negotiation frames it as joint problem-solving: we both win if we find a good solution. Negotiation jujitsu is the technique for shifting from the first frame to the second when the other side is operating within the contest frame. It works because most people are not committed positional bargainers by ideology; they bargain positionally because it is the only method they know. When offered a different frame — one that treats them with respect, takes their interests seriously, and invites collaborative problem-solving — many negotiators will shift, not because they have been persuaded by an argument, but because the new frame is more productive and less stressful.

## Negotiation Application

The practical execution of negotiation jujitsu requires discipline and composure. The natural human response to attack is fight or flight — to push back or to yield. Neither serves the negotiator's interests. Jujitsu requires a third response: curiosity. When the other side stakes out an extreme position, the principled negotiator responds not with outrage or capitulation but with genuine inquiry. This is harder than it sounds, because extreme positions trigger emotional reactions that make curiosity feel impossible. Fisher and Ury recommended a practice they borrowed from conflict resolution training: "going to the balcony" — mentally stepping back from the interaction to observe it from above before responding. This brief pause creates the space for a strategic response rather than an emotional reaction.

The technique is most effective when the other side's positional behavior is driven by habit, anxiety, or institutional culture rather than by genuine bad faith. A procurement officer who opens every negotiation with an extreme low offer may be following departmental policy rather than expressing a considered judgment. A lawyer who begins with aggressive demands may be performing for a client rather than revealing actual priorities. In these cases, negotiation jujitsu can be remarkably effective, because the positional behavior is a shell that cracks easily when met with interest-based engagement rather than reciprocal aggression.

Where jujitsu reaches its limits is with genuinely bad-faith actors — counterparts who are deliberately deceptive, who use tactics designed to manipulate or coerce, and who have no interest in a fair outcome. Fisher and Ury acknowledged this limitation and directed readers to BATNA as the ultimate protection. But the honest assessment is that negotiation jujitsu presupposes a minimum of good faith that is simply absent in some negotiations. Against a party that is truly committed to exploitation, the best protection is not technique but power — specifically, the power that comes from having a strong alternative.
