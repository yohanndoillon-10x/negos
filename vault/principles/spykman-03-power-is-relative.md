## The Principle

Spykman was emphatic on a point that many strategists and policymakers instinctively resist: power is never absolute. It is always and only relative. A state's power cannot be measured by adding up its GDP, counting its divisions, or tallying its nuclear warheads. It can only be assessed in relation to the power of the states it must contend with, in the specific geographic and political context in which competition occurs. A navy that dominates the Mediterranean may be irrelevant in the Pacific. An army that can defend Western Europe may be useless in the jungles of Southeast Asia. An economy that is the world's largest in aggregate may be overextended if its commitments exceed its capacity to sustain them.

This insistence on relativity was not merely a methodological preference. It was the foundation of Spykman's entire strategic framework. The Rimland thesis depends on relative power assessments: the question is not whether any single state is strong, but whether the balance of power along the Eurasian periphery is such that no single state or coalition can dominate. The United States does not need to be stronger than every potential adversary in every theater simultaneously. It needs to ensure that in the theaters that matter — the critical segments of the Rimland — the balance of power is favorable or at least not unfavorable.

Spykman extended this logic to the analysis of alliances. An alliance is not simply an aggregation of power; it is a relationship whose value depends on the geographic, military, and political context in which it operates. An ally that is strong in the wrong place is less valuable than an ally that is weak in the right place. An ally that is powerful but unreliable may be worse than useless — it may create false confidence that leads to strategic overextension. Relative power analysis requires examining not only the quantity of power but its quality, location, reliability, and applicability to the specific strategic problem at hand.

The corollary of relative power is that power can be increased not only by building up one's own resources but by diminishing, diverting, or constraining the resources of one's competitors. This is the logic of containment, of economic sanctions, of alliance-building that encircles an adversary and denies him access to the resources and relationships he needs. Spykman understood that in a competitive system, the ratio matters more than the total — and that the ratio can be shifted in your favor by actions that reduce the denominator as effectively as by actions that increase the numerator.

## Negotiation Application

The relativity of power is perhaps Spykman's single most directly applicable insight for negotiation. Negotiators routinely make the mistake of assessing their own leverage in isolation — they count their resources, their expertise, their authority, their willingness to walk away — without performing the critical comparative analysis. Your leverage is not what you have. It is what you have relative to what the other side has, needs, and can obtain elsewhere.

This reframing transforms negotiation preparation. Instead of asking "How strong am I?" the Spykman-informed negotiator asks: "How strong am I compared to my counterpart's alternatives?" The seller who has the only product on the market has enormous leverage — not because his product is inherently valuable, but because the buyer's alternatives are nonexistent. The same seller, facing five competitors with equivalent products, may have very little leverage despite offering exactly the same product. Nothing about the seller's absolute position has changed. Everything about his relative position has.

The practical discipline that follows is twofold. First, invest in understanding your counterpart's alternatives, constraints, and resources with the same rigor you apply to understanding your own. The negotiator who knows his own BATNA but not his counterpart's is operating with half a map. Second, take active steps to improve your relative position before the negotiation begins. This can mean developing your own alternatives (improving your BATNA), but it can equally mean actions that constrain your counterpart's alternatives — securing exclusive relationships, establishing precedents, creating switching costs, or building coalitions that reduce the other side's options.

Spykman's framework also warns against a common negotiation error: confusing absolute strength with relative leverage. The large corporation negotiating with the small supplier may assume it has overwhelming leverage simply because of its size. But if the supplier holds a patent, occupies a niche that no competitor serves, or has relationships that the corporation cannot easily replicate, the relative balance may be far more even than the absolute comparison suggests. The discipline of relative power assessment prevents the dangerous complacency that comes from assuming size alone determines outcomes.
