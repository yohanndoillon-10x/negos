## The Principle

BATNA — the Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement — is arguably Fisher and Ury's single most important conceptual contribution to the field of negotiation. While the four pillars of principled negotiation describe how to conduct a negotiation, BATNA answers a more fundamental question: whether to negotiate at all, and how much power you bring to the table when you do. Your BATNA is what you will do if the current negotiation fails to produce an agreement. It is not what you wish would happen, not your ideal outcome, and not a vague sense that you have other options. It is the specific, concrete course of action you will pursue if you walk away from the table.

Fisher and Ury's insight was that BATNA, not any other factor, determines your true negotiation power. A party with a strong BATNA — a genuinely attractive alternative to the deal on the table — can negotiate confidently, reject unfavorable proposals without anxiety, and credibly threaten to walk away. A party with a weak BATNA — no good alternative to the current negotiation — is structurally vulnerable, regardless of how skilled a negotiator she is. No technique, no clever argument, no amount of charisma can compensate for the absence of a real alternative. Conversely, a strong BATNA can compensate for almost any other negotiation weakness. The negotiator who can walk away has leverage; the negotiator who cannot, does not.

This reframing of negotiation power was genuinely revolutionary. In the popular imagination, and in much of the prior negotiation literature, power in negotiation was associated with size, wealth, status, toughness, or rhetorical skill. Fisher and Ury argued that all of these are secondary. The real question is always: "What happens if I don't make this deal?" If the answer is "something nearly as good," you are powerful. If the answer is "something catastrophic," you are not. The practical implication is transformative: instead of spending preparation time rehearsing arguments or planning tactical maneuvers, the principled negotiator spends it developing and improving her alternatives.

Fisher and Ury also introduced the complementary concept of the "reservation price" or "bottom line" — the worst deal you would accept, which should be determined by your BATNA. Any deal better than your BATNA is worth considering; any deal worse than your BATNA should be rejected. This provides a bright line for decision-making in the heat of negotiation, when psychological pressures — sunk costs, escalation of commitment, fear of losing the deal — can distort judgment. But Fisher and Ury cautioned against confusing BATNA with a rigid bottom line. A bottom line set in advance on the basis of arbitrary criteria can prevent a negotiator from accepting a deal that, while worse than hoped, is better than her alternatives. BATNA, because it is anchored in reality rather than aspiration, provides a more reliable compass.

## Negotiation Application

The most important practical application of BATNA is in preparation. Before any negotiation, the practitioner should complete three tasks. First, generate a list of possible alternatives if no agreement is reached — what will you actually do? Second, improve the most promising alternatives by taking concrete actions: soliciting competing offers, developing internal capabilities, exploring other partners, or preparing for litigation. Third, select the single best alternative and develop it to the point where you could execute it if necessary. This last step is critical. A BATNA that exists only as a theoretical possibility is not a BATNA; it is a fantasy. A real BATNA is one you have the capacity and the willingness to execute.

Equally important is assessing the other side's BATNA. If their alternatives are weak, your leverage is greater than your own BATNA alone would suggest. If their alternatives are strong, you need to either improve your own BATNA or find ways to make the deal more attractive to them. Fisher and Ury noted that parties often overestimate their own BATNA and underestimate the other side's — a cognitive bias that leads to unrealistic expectations and negotiation failure. Disciplined assessment of both sides' alternatives is the foundation of realistic negotiation strategy.

The principle's most significant limitation concerns power asymmetry. Fisher and Ury's advice to "improve your BATNA" is correct but can ring hollow when the power imbalance is structural. A small supplier negotiating with a dominant buyer, an employee negotiating with a monopoly employer, a developing country negotiating with a great power — in these cases, the weaker party's BATNA may be genuinely terrible and not meaningfully improvable. Fisher and Ury argued that principled negotiation can partially compensate — that even a weak party can achieve better outcomes by focusing on interests, inventing options, and invoking objective criteria — but their critics have argued, with some justice, that the framework underestimates the degree to which structural power shapes negotiation outcomes regardless of process. BATNA is the source of negotiation power, but not every party has the ability to build a strong one.
