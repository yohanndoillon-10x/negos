## The Principle

Zartman's formula-detail framework describes the two-phase structure through which successful negotiations proceed. In the first phase, the parties negotiate a formula — a shared framework of principles, trade-offs, and definitions of justice that establishes the conceptual foundation for an agreement. In the second phase, they work out the details — the specific terms, numbers, timelines, and implementation mechanisms that operationalize the formula. This distinction is not merely descriptive; it is analytically fundamental. Negotiations that attempt to resolve details before establishing a shared formula tend to bog down in positional bargaining, because the parties have no principled basis for evaluating concessions. Negotiations that establish a formula but cannot operationalize it in detail produce agreements that look impressive on paper but collapse in implementation.

The formula is, in essence, a shared definition of fairness — a mutually accepted framework for how the gains and losses of agreement will be distributed. It might take the form of a principle ("land for peace" in the Arab-Israeli context), a trade-off structure ("we give on tariffs, you give on intellectual property" in trade negotiations), or a procedural agreement ("all parties will have equal representation" in multilateral settings). The formula does not resolve the negotiation, but it creates the conceptual space within which resolution becomes possible. Without a formula, the parties are negotiating in a void — each proposal is evaluated in isolation, and there is no shared standard against which to measure whether a particular concession is fair or unfair, generous or inadequate.

The detail phase is where most negotiations encounter their greatest difficulties. A formula that seemed acceptable in principle often becomes contentious when translated into specific commitments. "Land for peace" is an elegant formula, but which land? What kind of peace? On what timeline? With what security guarantees? The detail phase tests whether the formula is robust enough to accommodate the parties' specific needs, and it is here that the negotiator's technical skill — the ability to craft language, design implementation mechanisms, and manage the myriad small decisions that constitute a complex agreement — becomes critical. Zartman observed that many negotiations fail not because the parties cannot agree on a formula but because they cannot agree on what the formula means in practice.

## Negotiation Application

The formula-detail framework provides a practical roadmap for structuring any complex negotiation. The first task is to invest adequate time in the formula phase — resisting the temptation to dive into specifics before the parties have agreed on the principles that will govern the exchange. In commercial negotiations, this means establishing the basic structure of the deal (What are we trading? What is the basis for valuation? How will risk be allocated?) before negotiating specific numbers. In organizational negotiations, it means agreeing on the criteria for decision-making before debating specific proposals. The formula phase is often the phase that impatient negotiators want to skip, and it is almost always the phase that determines whether the negotiation will succeed or fail.

The framework also explains a common pathology in negotiations: premature detail bargaining. When parties begin arguing about specific terms before establishing a shared formula, they tend to evaluate every proposal through the lens of their own interests and to treat every concession as a unilateral loss. The result is a grinding, positional dynamic in which progress is measured in millimeters and both sides feel that they are giving up more than they are getting. By contrast, when parties have agreed on a formula, each detail can be evaluated against a shared standard — "does this provision advance the principles we agreed to?" — and concessions become easier to make because they can be justified as consistent with the framework rather than as capitulations.

For mediators, the formula-detail framework suggests a specific sequence of interventions. In the early stages of a mediation, the priority is to help the parties discover or construct a shared formula — often by reframing their positions in terms of underlying interests and proposing trade-off structures that create value for both sides. Only once a formula is in place should the mediation shift to the detail phase, where the mediator's role becomes more technical and less conceptual. This sequencing helps prevent the common mediator mistake of trying to resolve specific disputes before the parties have agreed on the principles that should govern the resolution.
