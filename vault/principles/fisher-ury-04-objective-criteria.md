## The Principle

The fourth pillar of principled negotiation addresses the question of how to resolve conflicting interests without descending into a contest of wills. When two parties cannot agree on a price, a timeline, or a set of terms, positional bargaining provides only one mechanism for resolution: pressure. Each side pushes for its preferred outcome, and the final agreement reflects the balance of stubbornness, resources, and endurance rather than any standard of fairness or rationality. Fisher and Ury argued that this process is both inefficient and corrosive, and that a superior alternative exists: insist that the outcome be based on some objective standard independent of either side's will.

Objective criteria come in two forms: fair standards and fair procedures. Fair standards are external benchmarks that can be applied to the substantive question: market value, precedent, expert opinion, industry norms, legal principles, scientific measurement, replacement cost, depreciated book value. The crucial feature of these standards is that they are independent of what either side wants — they are facts about the world, not expressions of preference. Fair procedures are methods for resolving a dispute that are inherently neutral: taking turns, drawing lots, splitting the difference, using an independent appraiser, or employing the classic "one cuts, the other chooses" method. When substantive standards are unavailable or contested, a fair procedure can provide a resolution that both sides accept as legitimate.

The strategic logic of objective criteria is straightforward. A negotiator who anchors her proposals in external standards shifts the burden of argument to the other side. Instead of "I want X because it serves my interests," she says "X is what the market data indicate" or "X is consistent with what similar transactions have yielded." The other side cannot refute this by simply asserting a different preference — they must either accept the standard, propose a different standard that supports their position, or explain why the standard does not apply. The negotiation becomes a discussion about which criteria are appropriate rather than a contest of wills about which number is acceptable. This is not merely more pleasant; it is more efficient, because the range of defensible outcomes is typically much narrower than the range of assertable positions.

Fisher and Ury were careful to note that "objective" does not mean "indisputable." Any standard can be challenged. Market values depend on which comparables are selected. Expert opinions differ. Legal principles can be interpreted in multiple ways. The point is not that objective criteria produce mechanically correct answers, but that they provide a framework for reasoned discussion that is superior to pure bargaining power as a basis for agreement. A negotiation grounded in competing standards is substantively richer and more tractable than a negotiation grounded in competing demands.

## Negotiation Application

The most important application of this principle is in preparation. Before any negotiation, the practitioner should research and compile all relevant objective standards that support her position — and, equally important, anticipate which standards the other side is likely to invoke. The negotiation then becomes a structured discussion about which criteria are most appropriate, rather than a positional tug-of-war. This preparation also provides a powerful defense against anchoring: when the other side opens with an extreme position, the principled negotiator does not counter with an equally extreme position but instead asks, "What standard are you using to justify that number?" The question redirects the conversation from assertion to analysis.

The principle's weakness is that it assumes the existence of genuinely neutral criteria — an assumption that becomes strained in contexts where standards are inherently contested or culturally embedded. In many negotiations, the choice of which objective standard to apply is itself the substantive dispute. A seller who cites replacement cost and a buyer who cites depreciated book value are both invoking "objective criteria," but they are really fighting about which framework should govern the transaction. Fisher and Ury's advice — to be open to reason and to yield to principle but never to pressure — is sound, but it does not fully resolve the problem of competing frames. In practice, the negotiator who selects the governing standard often determines the outcome, which means that the battle over criteria is itself a negotiation that requires strategic skill.

Another practical challenge arises when the other side simply refuses to engage with objective criteria and insists on positional bargaining. Fisher and Ury counseled persistence — keep redirecting to standards, keep asking "what is the basis for that?" — but acknowledged that some counterparts will not be moved by rational argument. In these situations, BATNA becomes the operative principle: if the other side cannot be persuaded to negotiate on the merits, your alternative to a negotiated agreement is your only protection.
