## The Principle

Tacit bargaining is coordination without explicit communication. Schelling demonstrated that even when parties cannot talk — or choose not to — they can still reach mutually beneficial arrangements by relying on shared expectations, salient solutions, and the logic of mutual interest. The concept challenges the assumption that negotiation requires verbal exchange. In many situations, what is left unsaid is more strategically important than what is said.

Schelling explored tacit bargaining through a series of thought experiments. Two parachutists dropped in an area must find each other without radios. Two countries must establish a boundary without formal talks. Two competitors must coordinate pricing without illegal communication. In each case, the parties converge on focal points — outcomes that are salient, precedented, or psychologically prominent — because these are the only outcomes that both sides can independently identify as likely points of convergence. Tacit bargaining works precisely because it leverages the common knowledge that both parties share: knowledge of geography, convention, precedent, and human psychology.

The strategic power of tacit bargaining lies in its deniability and its economy. When parties reach an understanding without explicit agreement, neither can be accused of having made a deal. This is valuable in situations where explicit coordination would be illegal (antitrust), politically unacceptable (diplomatic back-channels), or personally risky (organizational politics). Tacit bargaining also avoids the transaction costs of formal negotiation — the posturing, the demands, the face-saving rituals — and allows parties to converge on efficient solutions without the friction of explicit exchange.

But tacit bargaining also has severe limitations. It works only when the parties share enough common context to identify the same focal points. When cultural backgrounds, institutional frameworks, or situational knowledge diverge, tacit coordination breaks down. And tacit bargaining is fragile: a small change in the environment — a new precedent, a shift in shared expectations — can displace the focal point and leave the parties unable to coordinate. The negotiator who relies exclusively on tacit understanding, without ever testing it through explicit communication, risks discovering too late that the other side was converging on a different focal point entirely.

## Negotiation Application

Tacit bargaining operates in every negotiation, whether the parties recognize it or not. The unspoken understandings about what is "reasonable," what precedent governs, what the "going rate" is — these are all products of tacit coordination. Skilled negotiators learn to read and manipulate tacit signals: the offer that is slightly below what the other side will accept signals willingness to negotiate; the long silence after a proposal signals dissatisfaction without making a counter-demand; the visible preparation for alternatives signals willingness to walk away without explicitly threatening to do so.

In multi-round negotiations, tacit bargaining is especially powerful. Patterns of concession and reciprocity create implicit norms that govern future interactions. A party that always reciprocates concessions builds a tacit expectation of reciprocity that the other side can rely on — and exploit. A party that never reciprocates destroys the tacit cooperative framework and forces the negotiation into an explicitly adversarial mode. Understanding these tacit dynamics allows the sophisticated negotiator to manage the tone and trajectory of a negotiation through actions rather than words.

The deepest application of tacit bargaining is knowing when not to speak. Sometimes the most powerful negotiation move is silence — allowing the other side to draw its own conclusions, form its own expectations, and make its own concessions without being asked. Schelling's framework teaches that explicit communication, while often necessary, can also be counterproductive: it can harden positions, trigger face-saving dynamics, and destroy the plausible deniability that makes compromise possible.
