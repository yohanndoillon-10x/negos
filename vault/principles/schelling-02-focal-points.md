## The Principle

Focal points — sometimes called Schelling points — are the natural coordination solutions that emerge when parties must agree without explicit communication, or when the range of possible agreements is so large that some principle of selection is needed to narrow it down. Schelling's most famous illustration is the coordination problem: if two people must meet somewhere in New York City on a given day but cannot communicate beforehand, where do they go? Experimentally, a striking majority converge on Grand Central Station at noon. There is nothing objectively optimal about this choice. Its power lies entirely in its salience — its psychological prominence as the "obvious" answer. Both parties choose it not because it is the best location, but because each expects the other to choose it, and each knows the other expects the same. The focal point is a self-fulfilling coordination device.

This insight has profound implications for negotiation. Whenever multiple agreements are possible and no party has the power to impose a specific outcome, focal points exert gravitational force on the result. Round numbers function as focal points in price negotiations: a salary negotiation that could rationally settle anywhere between $95,000 and $105,000 will disproportionately cluster around $100,000, not because that number has any special economic significance but because it is psychologically salient. Precedent functions as a focal point: whatever terms governed the last contract between two parties will strongly influence the next contract, because both sides recognize it as a natural starting point. Fifty-fifty splits are focal points in distributive bargaining. Existing boundaries — geographical, contractual, temporal — are focal points in territorial disputes. The status quo is perhaps the most powerful focal point of all: any proposed change must overcome the gravitational pull of the current arrangement.

Schelling recognized that focal points are culturally and contextually determined. What seems "obvious" to one party may not seem obvious to another, especially across cultural boundaries. A Western negotiator might regard an even split as the natural focal point in a joint venture; a counterpart from a culture that emphasizes seniority or relative contribution might regard a proportional split as equally natural. The power of a focal point depends on shared perception, and where perceptions diverge, the focal point weakens or disappears. This means that the ability to establish or shift focal points is a genuine form of strategic power. The party that can frame the negotiation so that a particular outcome appears natural, obvious, or inevitable has done most of the work of achieving that outcome.

The mechanism through which focal points operate is expectational convergence. Each party asks: "What will the other side expect me to propose? What do they expect I expect them to propose?" This chain of mutual expectations rapidly converges on outcomes that are prominent, simple, or precedented — not because anyone has argued for them, but because they are the only outcomes that both sides can independently identify as the likely point of agreement. In this sense, focal points are as much about psychology and shared knowledge as they are about the substance of the negotiation.

## Negotiation Application

The practical lesson is that framing and anchoring are not merely tactical ploys — they are mechanisms for establishing focal points. The negotiator who can make a particular number, structure, or precedent seem like the natural outcome of the negotiation has gained a powerful advantage. This is why the first offer matters so much: it establishes a reference point around which subsequent discussion gravitates. It is why invoking precedent ("the industry standard is...") or external benchmarks ("the market rate is...") is effective: these references create focal points that the other side must actively resist.

Sophisticated negotiators also learn to displace unfavorable focal points. If the status quo favors the other side, introducing a new framework — a different comparable, a different metric, a different precedent — can shift the focal point and with it the expected outcome. Schelling's analysis suggests that the battle over focal points is often the decisive negotiation, more important than the substantive arguments that follow. The party that controls the frame controls the focal point, and the party that controls the focal point controls the range of likely outcomes.
