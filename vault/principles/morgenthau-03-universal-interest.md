## The Principle

Morgenthau's third principle addresses a potential objection to his own framework: if all political behavior is driven by interest defined as power, does this not produce a rigid, ahistorical theory that ignores the vast differences between political cultures, eras, and contexts? His answer is that the concept of interest defined as power is universally valid as a *category* of analysis, but the specific *content* of that category varies enormously. What counts as "interest" and what counts as "power" are not fixed — they are shaped by the political and cultural environment in which statesmen operate.

This is a crucial refinement that separates Morgenthau from cruder forms of realism. He is not claiming that all states want the same things or that power always takes the same form. A medieval king's interest was dynastic succession and territorial control; a modern democratic leader's interest includes domestic political survival, economic performance, and alliance management. Power in the eighteenth century meant armies and navies; power in the twenty-first century includes technological infrastructure, financial networks, and information control. The grammar of interest and power is constant; the vocabulary changes with every era.

This principle also reflects Morgenthau's intellectual debt to Max Weber's insistence on understanding social action from within its own cultural framework — *Verstehen*. Morgenthau understood that a Chinese diplomat, an American president, and a Nigerian general do not conceptualize power in identical ways. Their cultural, institutional, and historical contexts shape how they define their interests and what instruments of power they consider legitimate and effective. A theory of international politics that ignores this variation is not rigorous; it is provincial, projecting one culture's assumptions onto a universal framework.

Yet the underlying dynamic remains. Regardless of how a particular actor defines "interest" or "power," the logic of pursuing interest through the accumulation and exercise of power is observable across every political system humans have created. This is what gives the theory its explanatory power. You can study Thucydides' account of the Melian Dialogue and Bismarck's management of European alliances and the Cold War arms race using the same analytical framework — not because these situations are identical, but because the actors in each case are pursuing interest defined as power, expressed in the idiom of their time.

Morgenthau also uses this principle to guard against ideological rigidity within realism itself. A realist theory that cannot account for change — that assumes power always means military power, or that national interest never evolves — is as dogmatic as the idealism it criticizes. By insisting that the content of interest and power is historically contingent, Morgenthau built flexibility into the heart of his framework. Realism, properly understood, is not a fixed set of conclusions about what states want. It is a method of analysis that takes power seriously while remaining alert to how power transforms itself across time and context.

## Negotiation Application

For negotiators, this principle is a warning against projection. The most common analytical error in cross-cultural or cross-organizational negotiation is assuming that the other side defines "interest" and "power" the same way you do. A Western corporate negotiator who assumes his Chinese counterpart prioritizes short-term profitability in the same way may miss entirely that the counterpart's operational definition of interest centers on long-term market position, government relationships, or organizational prestige.

This principle demands that negotiators invest in understanding the other side's frame of reference before the negotiation begins. What does power look like in their context? What currency do they trade in — formal authority, relational capital, information asymmetry, institutional access? The answers will vary, and the negotiator who assumes universality where specificity is required will misread signals, misinterpret offers, and craft proposals that fail to address what the other side actually values.

At the same time, the universality of the *category* — the fact that everyone, everywhere, in some form, is pursuing interest defined as power — provides a common analytical language. Even when the content differs, the structure is the same. The negotiator who grasps this can enter any negotiation and begin by asking: what is the interest here, and what power backs it? The specific answers will change. The question never does.
