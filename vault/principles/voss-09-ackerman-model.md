## The Principle

The Ackerman model is Voss's systematic approach to the distributive dimension of negotiation — the part where you are haggling over a specific number, whether it is price, salary, rent, or any other quantifiable term. While most of Voss's system addresses the emotional and informational dimensions of negotiation, the Ackerman model provides a structured procedure for the moment when the conversation turns to numbers. It is a six-step process designed to anchor aggressively, make calibrated concessions, and signal finality — all while keeping emotional rapport intact.

The steps are as follows. First, set your target price — the amount you actually want to pay (or receive). Second, calculate three incremental offers at 65, 85, and 95 percent of your target price (if buying) or the corresponding inverse percentages (if selling). Third, open with the 65 percent offer, accompanied by an empathetic accusation audit to soften the anchor's impact. Fourth, when the counterpart rejects the first offer and counters, use calibrated questions and labels to slow the process and gather information before moving to your second offer at 85 percent. Fifth, move to your third offer at 95 percent, again preceded by calibrated questions and emotional engagement. Sixth, make your final offer at 100 percent — your target price — and make it a precise, non-round number (for example, $37,893 rather than $38,000), accompanied by a small non-monetary concession to signal that you have reached your absolute limit.

The psychological architecture of the Ackerman model is carefully designed. The decreasing increments — from 65 to 85 (a 20-point jump), from 85 to 95 (a 10-point jump), from 95 to 100 (a 5-point jump) — signal to the counterpart that the negotiator is approaching their limit. Each successive concession is smaller, creating the impression of someone who is being squeezed dry. The precise final number reinforces this impression: a round number suggests an arbitrary anchor; a precise number suggests a figure that has been carefully calculated from real constraints. And the non-monetary concession at the end — "I'll throw in two months of free support" — signals generosity while simultaneously communicating that monetary movement is over.

The model's genius lies in the way it integrates Voss's emotional techniques with the hard mechanics of distributive bargaining. The initial extreme anchor is delivered with an accusation audit ("I know this is going to sound crazy...") that prevents the counterpart from taking offense. Each subsequent offer is preceded by calibrated questions ("How am I supposed to make this work at that price?") that force the counterpart to justify their counter and reveal information. The final precise number, paired with a non-monetary sweetener, creates a psychologically compelling endpoint. The counterpart does not feel beaten — they feel that they have pushed the negotiator to their genuine limit, which is a form of victory that preserves the relationship.

## Negotiation Application

The Ackerman model requires disciplined preparation. Before the negotiation, the practitioner must determine their target price based on research, alternatives, and genuine constraints — not wishful thinking. The three calculated offers (65/85/95 percent) must be ready before the conversation begins, because calculating in real time under the pressure of negotiation invites errors and reveals uncertainty. The non-monetary concession to accompany the final offer should also be prepared in advance: something of genuine value to the counterpart that costs the negotiator relatively little.

The most important implementation detail is pacing. The Ackerman model is designed to be slow. Each offer should be separated by genuine engagement — mirroring, labeling, calibrated questions — that serves the dual purpose of gathering information and demonstrating that the concession was painful and hard-won. A negotiator who runs through all four offers in rapid succession has destroyed the model's psychological architecture. The counterpart needs to experience each concession as a struggle — as something they extracted through skill and persistence — in order for the model to produce a stable agreement. Rushed concessions signal that there is more room to move.

The non-round final number deserves special attention. Research in negotiation and behavioral economics consistently demonstrates that precise numbers are perceived as more credible and more anchored in reality than round numbers. A buyer who offers $127,500 is perceived as having done their homework; a buyer who offers $130,000 is perceived as having picked a convenient number. This perception matters because it affects how the counterpart responds. A precise number discourages further negotiation because it implies a specific, calculated limit. A round number invites further negotiation because it implies approximation and flexibility.

Common mistakes with the Ackerman model include three categories. First, opening too aggressively without the emotional cushion of an accusation audit, which causes the counterpart to disengage or respond with hostility. Second, making concessions too quickly, which signals weakness and encourages the counterpart to push for more. Third, failing to use the non-monetary concession at the final step, which means the negotiator has no way to signal finality except through words — and words alone are rarely credible. The non-monetary concession is not optional decoration; it is a functional element that communicates "I have no more money to give, but I want to make this work."
