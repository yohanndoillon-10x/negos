## The Principle

Morgenthau's sixth principle completes the architectural logic of his framework by reasserting the autonomy of political thought in its strongest form. While the second principle distinguishes the political sphere from other domains, the sixth goes further: it insists that in matters of political decision-making, political criteria must take precedence over all others. The economist may evaluate a policy by its efficiency, the lawyer by its legality, the ethicist by its conformity with moral principles. The political analyst — and, by extension, the statesman — must evaluate it by a single overriding question: does it serve the political interest of the actor?

This is the principle that has drawn the most criticism of Morgenthau's work, and the one that is most commonly misunderstood. Critics read it as a license for amorality — as a claim that power is all that matters and that legal, moral, and economic considerations can be discarded at will. This is a misreading. Morgenthau is not saying that these considerations are unimportant. He is saying that they must be *subordinated* to political analysis, not substituted for it. A policy that is economically optimal but politically disastrous is a bad policy. A policy that is legally impeccable but politically naive is a bad policy. A policy that is morally admirable but politically impossible is worse than useless — it creates expectations that cannot be met and commitments that cannot be honored.

The intellectual context for this principle is Morgenthau's ongoing argument with what he called the "legalistic-moralistic" approach to international relations. This approach, which he associated with Woodrow Wilson and the entire tradition of liberal internationalism, assumed that legal frameworks and moral norms could substitute for political judgment. Morgenthau had watched this assumption fail catastrophically in the interwar period. The Kellogg-Briand Pact of 1928 outlawed war. It did not prevent war, because outlawing something is a legal act, and political realities do not yield to legal acts unless those acts are backed by political power. The lesson Morgenthau drew was not that law is worthless, but that law without political backing is theater — and dangerous theater, because it creates an illusion of security where none exists.

This principle also reflects Morgenthau's understanding of the distinctive burden of political decision-making. The economist, the lawyer, and the moralist each operate within a single evaluative framework. The statesman must integrate all of these frameworks — and then make a decision under conditions of uncertainty, with incomplete information, against time pressure, knowing that the consequences will be measured in human lives. In this context, Morgenthau argues, the statesman who subordinates political judgment to any single non-political criterion has abdicated his responsibility. He has allowed one dimension of reality to crowd out all the others, producing a decision that is technically sound in one domain and catastrophic in the one that matters most: the domain of power and its consequences.

## Negotiation Application

For negotiators, this principle translates into a hierarchy of analysis. Legal arguments, financial models, ethical frameworks, and technical assessments are all valuable inputs. But the decision — the actual choice of what to propose, what to accept, and what to reject — must be driven by a political assessment: where does the power lie, what are the consequences of each option for my position, and what outcome is achievable given the real balance of forces?

This is particularly relevant in complex, multi-stakeholder negotiations where different team members bring different professional lenses. The lawyer on your team may insist on a clause that is legally defensible but politically provocative. The finance lead may push for terms that are economically optimal but that the other side cannot politically accept. The negotiation lead must exercise what Morgenthau would recognize as political judgment: integrating these perspectives while keeping the political objective — the outcome that serves your overall interest given the power dynamics in play — at the center.

The principle also warns against a specific negotiation failure mode: the tendency to let process substitute for strategy. Negotiators who focus excessively on procedural fairness, legal compliance, or technical correctness can lose sight of the political objective. The question is not "is this process fair?" but "is this process producing an outcome that reflects and protects my interests?" Morgenthau's subordination principle keeps the negotiator focused on what actually determines success: power, interest, and consequence.
