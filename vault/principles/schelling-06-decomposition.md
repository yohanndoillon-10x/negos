## The Principle

Decomposition is the strategy of breaking a complex, multi-issue conflict into a series of smaller, sequential negotiations. Schelling observed that large, all-or-nothing bargaining situations tend to produce deadlock, escalation, or catastrophically suboptimal outcomes, because the stakes are too high for either side to make concessions and the complexity is too great for either side to evaluate the full package. By decomposing the conflict into smaller pieces, each manageable in isolation, the parties can build momentum through incremental agreements, establish trust through repeated reciprocity, and reduce the risk associated with any single concession.

The logic of decomposition rests on several reinforcing mechanisms. First, smaller stakes lower the emotional and strategic barriers to agreement. A concession on a minor issue is easier to make and easier to justify internally than a concession on the central issue. Second, sequential agreements create precedent and momentum. Each successful resolution establishes a pattern of cooperation that makes the next resolution more likely. Third, decomposition allows the parties to learn about each other — about preferences, constraints, and credibility — through low-stakes interactions before confronting the high-stakes issues. Fourth, by separating issues that are linked only by the accident of proximity, decomposition prevents one contentious issue from poisoning agreement on all the others.

Schelling applied this logic most explicitly to arms control, where the history of failed comprehensive agreements suggested that a step-by-step approach — limited test bans, specific weapons categories, incremental verification measures — was more likely to produce results than grand bargains that tried to resolve everything at once. The same logic applies to any negotiation where the full package is too complex, too contentious, or too high-stakes for a single round of bargaining. Mergers and acquisitions, peace processes, multi-year commercial contracts, and organizational restructurings all benefit from decomposition when the alternative is deadlock or collapse.

The risk of decomposition is that it can produce piecemeal outcomes that lack coherence, or that early agreements on easy issues leave only the hardest issues for the end, when the pressure to agree has diminished and each remaining issue becomes a potential deal-breaker. Schelling's framework acknowledges this risk and implies that the sequencing of issues is itself a strategic decision. The negotiator who structures the sequence — placing issues in an order that builds trust, creates momentum, and reserves enough mutual benefit for the final rounds — is practicing decomposition at its most sophisticated.

## Negotiation Application

In practice, decomposition means resisting the temptation to negotiate everything at once. Instead of presenting a comprehensive proposal and debating its merits line by line, the decomposition-oriented negotiator identifies which issues can be resolved independently and tackles them first. This creates a track record of agreement, generates goodwill, and — critically — makes it psychologically harder for either side to walk away from the negotiation, because doing so would mean abandoning the gains already achieved.

The technique is particularly valuable in high-conflict situations where trust is low. When parties cannot agree on the whole, they can often agree on a piece. And the act of agreeing — even on something small — changes the dynamic. It shifts the relationship from pure adversarialism to one in which cooperation has been demonstrated and can be referenced. Schelling's decomposition principle teaches that the path to resolving a difficult negotiation often begins not with the most important issue but with the most tractable one.
