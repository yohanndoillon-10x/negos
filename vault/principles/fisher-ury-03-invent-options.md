## The Principle

The third pillar of principled negotiation targets the assumption that most undermines creative problem-solving in bargaining: the belief that the pie is fixed. Most negotiators approach the table with the implicit premise that there is a defined amount of value to be divided, and that any gain for one side must come at the other's expense. This zero-sum mindset is so deeply embedded in how people think about negotiation that they rarely question it — and it is almost always wrong, or at best radically incomplete. Fisher and Ury argued that in nearly every negotiation, there are unexplored options that could increase the total value available to both sides, and that the failure to discover these options is a failure of imagination, not a fact about the world.

The obstacles to creative option generation are both psychological and structural. Fisher and Ury identified four specific barriers. First, premature judgment — the tendency to evaluate ideas as soon as they are proposed, which kills creative thinking before it can develop. Second, searching for the single answer — the assumption that there is one right solution and that the negotiator's job is to find it and fight for it. Third, the assumption of a fixed pie — the zero-sum mindset described above. Fourth, thinking that "solving their problem is their problem" — the belief that a negotiator's only responsibility is to advance her own interests, with no obligation to consider whether the other side's needs could be met in ways that do not cost her anything.

Fisher and Ury prescribed a deliberate process for overcoming these barriers. The first step is to separate inventing from deciding — to create a space in which ideas can be generated freely, without the pressure of commitment. This is the negotiation equivalent of brainstorming. The ground rules are explicit: no idea is evaluated, no proposal is binding, and the purpose is to expand the range of possibilities, not to narrow it. The second step is to broaden the options on the table rather than searching for a single solution. Fisher and Ury recommended looking through the lens of different experts, using the "circle chart" to move systematically between the specific problem and general approaches, and generating multiple proposals that each address the parties' interests in different ways. The third step is to look for mutual gains — to identify areas where the parties' interests are shared or complementary, and to design options that exploit these alignments. The fourth step is to make the other side's decision easy — to structure proposals so that the path of least resistance for the other side leads to the outcome you want.

The principle draws on a foundational insight from negotiation theory: differences between the parties are not obstacles to agreement — they are the raw material from which agreements are constructed. Differences in valuation ("I value this asset more than you do"), differences in time preference ("I need money now; you need money later"), differences in risk tolerance ("I can absorb this risk more easily than you can"), and differences in prediction ("I think the market will go up; you think it will go down") — all of these create opportunities for trades that make both parties better off. The skilled negotiator does not try to eliminate differences. She exploits them to create value.

## Negotiation Application

The most practical application of this principle is the disciplined use of brainstorming in negotiation preparation. Before any meeting, the principled negotiator should generate at least five to ten options that could address both sides' interests. These options should vary in structure — some should be comprehensive packages, others should be partial agreements on specific issues, and at least one or two should be deliberately unconventional. The purpose is not to identify the "right" answer in advance but to arrive at the table with a rich inventory of possibilities that can be adapted in real time as the other side's interests become clearer.

The principle's limitation is that it works best when both sides are willing to engage in collaborative problem-solving. Against a purely positional bargainer — someone who views every suggestion as a concession and responds to brainstorming with anchoring — the inventing-options approach can feel like unilateral disarmament. Fisher and Ury acknowledged this challenge and directed readers to BATNA as the protective mechanism. But the deeper issue is that option generation requires a minimum of trust and goodwill. In negotiations characterized by deep distrust, the process of generating joint options may need to be mediated by a neutral third party — which is precisely the situation that the one-text procedure was designed to address.

A common practitioner mistake is confusing option generation with concession-making. Generating multiple options is not the same as making multiple offers. Options are proposals for discussion, not commitments. The negotiator who presents five options is not signaling flexibility on all five — she is inviting the other side to reveal their preferences, which provides critical information about underlying interests. The distinction is subtle but essential: options are tools for exploration, not signals of willingness to concede.
